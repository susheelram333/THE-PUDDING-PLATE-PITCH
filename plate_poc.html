What You Can't Say

What's too offensive to bolt onto your car is decided state by state. The denied lists are a negative-space portrait of what each government polices — and they barely agree with each other.

How we did this

Data. Two real corpora. (1) ~27 states' banned-plate lists obtained via public-records requests (governmentattic.org / MuckRock, 2012–13), of which we use 11 with state-specific, plausibly-sized lists. (2) 23,463 California review applications (2015–16, Veltman/DMV) that carry the actual approve/deny verdict — the source of every "verifiably allowed" string. Current lists (TX 2025: 1,951; IL cumulative 8,558+; FL 2024: 209) anchor the present-day layer.

What we excluded, and why. Three "states" (AZ, DC, VT) returned 22k–26k entries that are ~100% mutually contained — a tiny jurisdiction like DC cannot have 25,000 of its own rejections. These are shared vendor screening lists, not state rejections, so we dropped them. Disclosing this is the point: the contradiction rate is computed only on lists we trust.

The honest limits. "Banned by one state" includes strings that may be banned-but-unpublished elsewhere — so Section 3's 88.3% is an upper bound on inconsistency; the 35 verified California contradictions are the floor. A keyword codebook catches only ~17% of what California's human reviewers flagged, because applicants disguise meaning (leetspeak, foreign words, phonetics) — itself part of the story.
