# THE-PUDDING-PLATE-PITCH
One-sentence point: What's too offensive to bolt onto a car is decided state by state, and the fifty maps of American taboo barely agree with each other.
Every state's DMV keeps a list of vanity plates it has rejected, and that denied pile is a negative-space portrait of what each government is afraid to see on a bumper. Read across states, the lists don't line up: of 24,412 unique banned strings from eleven states, 88.3% are rejected by only one state, and just 4.8% are banned by three or more. The shared American taboo turns out to be tiny and obvious — FUCK, SHIT, ASS, KILL — and everything past that thin core is contested terrain. The cleanest proof isn't absence but verdict: thirty-five strings California approved are banned elsewhere, and the standout is GAY — issued in California, rejected in New York, Kansas, Iowa, and Colorado. That's the whole piece in three letters: not a profanity fight, a values fight. The map has real fingerprints, too — most states police profanity or sex first, but New Jersey's number-one rejection category is impersonating the police. And the denied pile is a clock: COVFEFE in 2017, LET'S GO BRANDON in 2021, Hawk Tuah in 2024, ICE GANG and JAIL 45 in 2025 — each year's argument shows up on the plates.
The aha is that a license plate is a tiny, government-rationed unit of public speech, and the rejection lists are the most granular record we have of where each state draws the line between expression and offense — a line that moves the moment you cross a state border. It reveals something true about how American values are administered: not by one rule, but by fifty quietly different ones, applied by clerks, and increasingly fought over in court (the question is live at the Supreme Court as of December 2025).
It's a visual essay because the argument is a map and a sorting machine, not a sentence. The whiteboard drawing: type any string into a plate-shaped input and watch the country react — states stamp DENIED in red or clear it in green, with a running tally of how many of them would reject you. Below that, a tile-grid map of the country colored by the single category each state polices most, so the regional fingerprints read at a glance. Then the seismograph: the denied pile sorted by year, the national mood legible in the rejections. The personal mechanic — type your own plate, or your name, and see who bans it — is the shareable engine.
The data is already in hand, which is what makes this real rather than hopeful. Two public corpora: eleven states' rejection lists from public-records requests, and 23,463 California review applications that carry the actual approve/deny verdict — the source of every "verifiably allowed" string. A working prototype already does the real lookups across twelve states. The honesty is built in: "banned by one state" is an upper bound on inconsistency, the thirty-five California contradictions are the iron-clad floor, and the tool tells you absence isn't approval. Three "states" that turned out to be shared vendor screening lists were caught and excluded — which is the rigor, not a flaw.
It's evergreen, it carries a clear point of view without chasing the news cycle, and it's the kind of thing you can't unsee once you've typed your own name into it.
